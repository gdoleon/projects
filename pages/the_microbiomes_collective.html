The Microbiomes
Collective

2017

The Microbiomes Collective is a speculative platform that makes visible the biological connection we have with spaces. Through the use of a handheld device and a mobile application, it displays how our movement through the city affects the balance of our health in relation to usually unseen microbiomes.

Approach

Research and Development

Outcome

Duration


7 months



About the project


The Microbiome is the community of microorganisms that belong to a particular environment, inside and outside of our bodies. They surround the spaces we inhabit.

The presence of microorganisms in these microbiomes have both beneficial andadverse effects on our physical health. The accelerated growth of big cities is altering the natural balance of these microbiomes, transforming cities into potentially hostile habitats. How would you navigate the city every day knowing the biological impact that different spaces have on our bodies?

The Microbiome Collective is a speculative platform that makes visible the biological connection we have with spaces. Through the use of a handheld device and a mobile application, it visualises how our movement through the city influences our health by displaying normally unseen microbiomes. This platform suggests a different way of seeing urban spaces, opening possibilities for exploration and access to our personal health.

Approach   


This phase of the MA program was structured in four directions. These design directions were: design for enterprise, design for public systems, service design, and design for discourse. Students were asked to focus on one of these four different directions as a foundation for their projects. The method used throughout the development of the thesis project encouraged self-directed research backed up by professional mentoring and studio reviews.



Speculative Critical Design


My engagement with discursive methods through previous work within the MA program shaped the direction of my project from early stages. I realised that this channel could allow me to address a near future scenario (Taxonomy of Futures, Dunne & Raby) where present technology could be pushed to tackle growing environmental concerns and open new ways of interfacing with our environment.

Research & Development   


Through the process of looking for sustainable systems, alternative manufacturing and nature-based processes, I found interest in areas such as sports, outdoor activities, energy, biotechnology and synergistic systems. While exploring synergistic systems I learned how bacteria, which are also part of our immune systems, are directly connected to our health. This was the starting point for the refinement of my thesis statement.

The project proposed a crowd-sourced platform for the exploration of the microbial world. It was directed to citizen scientists. This group of citizens included people that were willing to collaborate towards the expansion of scientific knowledge.

Looking at the advancement in sequencing technologies and the miniaturisation of laboratory equipment as the basis of the project, this platform proposed a cloud infrastructure that could process samples of different materials collected by users to then respond through an interface that indicated the types of organisms they had found, where they were located and how they were related to their health.

Building Scenarios


How would a device like this be used within the context of urban life? And how could you display all this information to the user? Although very broad, these thought exercises started directing me to the construction of a visual language and framework for the service. An important aspect of these stories was that through this technology I was empowering the users to read their environment in different ways.

Part of the concept at this stage aimed to design a modular system that allowed you to have different intensities of monitoring and offered flexibility in its use. However, these directions required a high understanding of gene sequencing technologies for the proper functioning of the device.



Technical Feasibility


Through the research phase of the project I came across a series of technologies and research papers that studied and speculated about the feasibility of miniaturising sequencing technologies. A company called Oxford Nanopore Technologies had already developed the smallest DNA and RNA sequencer devices ever created. These devices offer almost real-time data of DNA and RNA molecules. These are devices that exist today and have a smaller form factor than a smartphone. This led me to believe that in the near future and with further development this technology could be iterated, improved and miniaturised more giving birth to a new range of sequencing devices.

Searching for a Typology


Looking into different shapes but following a simple and minimalistic language, I sketched the different ways that this speculative object could take, always considering the technological components inside the device.

Prototyping


3D modelling allowed me to quickly test the feasibility of the concepts as well as the practicality. Through these concepts I wanted to speculate on the shape and internal architecture of the device.

Outcome   


The visual interface is what ties the concept together. For the goals of this project I emphasised the visual language inside the app to reinforce a clear communication to the user. I also focused on the whole architecture of the system. I defined the medium for interactions and built a visual language that ties together every piece of the service.

Visual Language


The majority of people’s perception of the planet is ruled by what is visible and what can be immediately perceived. Factors such as climate change and health are things that can be invisible to the naked eye. This project’s first aim was to speculate about how design can help visualise these invisible forces.

The microbiome is not one microbe but a network of them that work synergistically with its host, be it human, animal or spatial. I started to ideate with 3D elements to find a way to visualise the balance of our surrounding microbiomes.

Chemicals


Faceted, geometric bodies are used to identify chemicals found around different environments in the city. The faces of these bodies reflect the number of substances and chemicals that have been found through scanning with the device.

Organisms


Organic, liquid-like bodies are used to visualise the library of organisms found in different environments around the city. This houses both beneficial and harmful microorganisms.

Indicator


The organic shape of the indicator body resembles a liquid element. Through this metaphor I wanted to communicate how vulnerable our bodies are to the environment around us. This indicator reacts to the user’s input and fluctuates between an altered and a balanced state. The less diversity of bacteria the users find the more altered the object becomes. Viruses and chemicals also affect the shape negatively. The user has the challenge to keep the object balanced (in a calmed and still state) by exploring their environment.

UI


Through the user interface I wanted to tell the story of a user that moves and commutes through the city having a visual understanding of its microbiomes and diversity of organisms. I was asking myself, how would someone change their behaviour knowing where beneficial and harmful organisms live?

The Home Screen


Graphic indicator that responds to the microorganisms and chemicals found. The more harmful organisms the user finds the more indicator will alter its shape.

The bacterial score is a synthesis of the balance of the organisms the users have found. An algorithm processes these findings with online data to establish a score that represents the balance of the user’s health.

The assistant acts as an artificial intelligent bot that crosses data gathered by different users across the city, to suggest activities that can improve the user’s bacterial score.





Map View


The Threats label shows the organisms found that present a threat to the user’s health. A geotag is created for each finding. These could take the form of viruses and chemicals.

The Unknown label is a record of unknown findings. Biological data that couldn’t be classified. It could represent a new organism or error.

The Organic label represents the list of organisms found during the day.

Sampling


Through the main home screen the user can access the active sampling mode. This mode would trigger the sampling device and pop up a "scanning" animation for the duration of the sampling. I thought it was important to visually show the user what was happening during the sampling scanning.

For the animation I took inspiration from particles moving in the air and agglomerating together, in the centre of the screen, as a way of showing them being captured by the device.

Architecture


This exploded view aims to show a simple internal layout of how components could be arranged. The top of the device would host the hardware related to mechanics and acquiring the sample. The middle section would host the nanopore sensor and the lower part of the device would host the battery.

Sampling Mechanics


  1. The propeller spins creating a vacuum-like force that forces airborne particles inside.
  2. The air sample passes through the first chamber.
  3. The nanopore sensor captures the particles and starts analysis.
  4. Data is transmitted to the phone.

Using Passive Tracking


While on passive tracking the device runs on the background taking samples as the user moves through spaces. While it runs in passive mode it geotags each sample and plots it on the map.





Using Active Tracking


Active tracking can be triggered at any time by the user if needed. This mode works as a normal sampling action that records the particles found as well as the location.
