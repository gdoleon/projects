Aptus

2017

Aptus is a medical handheld haemoglobin device that uses a drop of blood to read haematocrit and haemoglobin levels.

Brand

UI

About the project


Aptus is an iteration of technology developed at Entia which at the time was called Eva Diagnostics. Aptus built on top of an early working prototype that looked at haematocrit and haemoglobin.

During my time in this project our design team had already gathered feedback from field testing with core users. These insights led to the physical redesign of the product. My role as a designer focused on UX and packaging aspects as well as participation in creating a brand identity.


Brand   


After having informed myself on the vision and objectives of the company we proceeded to ideate and explore a renewed brand direction.

After a series of workshops and collaborative sessions we started to capture the identity of the different concepts created through logos, exploring shapes and curves as isotypes and imagetypes.

Packaging Exploration


With packaging we took the opportunity to explore a range of applications. I built different concepts ranging from simple low cost solutions to higher end concepts that explored a different market. This diverging phase was important for the brand as it helped refine its identity and consider future opportunities.

Carry Case


The nature of the project meant that the device could be carried around easily and reach distant locations where laboratory equipment couldn't reach. This meant the device needed protection and a way of transporting its components.

Through the ideation phase I started exploring the ways its components could be hosted within one protective case. The result was a bespoke carry case that enclosed all the needed parts for the device. I worked closely with UK and Chinese manufacturing suppliers to build and iterate through different concepts until we arrived at a good solution.

Manual


Part of the user experience was being able to access all the needed information to operate and troubleshoot the device. We had to include 3 different languages, and regulatory information was also needed as part of a comprehensive set of instructions for use.

I put emphasis in the visuals and illustrations to be able to convey the right information to the user. We prioritised the quality of visual information over text.

Cuvettes


The device used plastic consumables to collect the blood sample. They were provided to the user in bags of 50 units. The cuvettes packaging was kept minimal and simple to reduce costs.

User Interface   


The user interface had to be built from scratch. I put emphasis on having a cohesive wireframe that was iterated through testing with health professionals to ensure the information conveyed had the right wording and specificity. All icons and buttons were constructed by scratch and tested for fidelity and resolution. We worked with a low resolution screen which meant paying attention to pixel perfect artwork and assets.


The icons for each of the features of the device were drawn and explored through hand sketches and iterated with inverted colours in order to test a dark theme version with users.

The different screens were also prototyped and tested to assure that all active areas were correctly mapped and to confirm they had the right size and were accessible by users. At this point different UI concepts were tested, including a multicolour icon UI.


A bespoke numerical keypad was part of an early concept for clinical scenarios. Through this feature health professionals could be able to record the results against a patient ID number in order to better access past results. We paid attention at the minimum active area size and tested various concepts to make sure all buttons were accessible.

Through the interface the user could set up the device with their demographic information. This allowed the device to set the correct reference range for each user. The device could allow health professionals to change the references used and the cutoff values for both haemoglobin and haematocrit.
