Liberty

2021

Liberty is a home-based, point of care device that assists cancer patients by allowing them to monitor their health and blood counts from home. The device is part of a wider platform that connects doctors and nurses with patients.

Brief


As a mission, the project aimed to improve the quality of life of immunocompromised cancer patients.

Using technology developed by Entia we set the aim of designing a testing platform that could allow patients to be monitored from home, reducing the time spent in waiting rooms and also the risks that come with exposing themselves to crowds and spaces.

Cancer affects people disregarding age or sex, for that reason our sample of users had a diverse demographic. We focused at designing inclusively, paying attention at having elderly patients present throughout our studies allowed us to design a system that could be adopted by most users.



Role


Senior Industrial & UX Designer.

Approach   


The project was structured having patients at the heart of the development process. We followed a medical device development approach and started by conducting collaborative sessions to understand the current state of cancer treatment, identify challenges and highlight opportunities.



Co-Design Studies


We structured the discovery phase to be a collaborative process between every stakeholder involved.

The research sessions and focus groups were designed in a way that allowed each stakeholder to bring their own view and experience on what the challenges were. We ran mixed sessions were doctors would seat next to patients and nurses and other independent sessions with each stakeholder. These sessions were conducted at partner hospitals across London and included cancer patients, carers, nurses, doctors and consultants.

Research & Co-Creation   


This phase allowed us to create a comprehensive map of the entire cancer treatment cycle. This mapping made the existing hospital workstreams visible and highlighted the problems and opportunities to our team.

Storyboarding Early Concepts


Through the collaborative discovery process we could better define the limits of the platform. The platform needed to allow the patient to perform a blood test from home using a finger prick. The blood sample would then be analysed by the device and the results sent to the hospital wirelessly. This early scenario allowed us to look at the different touch points across the user journey and speculate on the different ways we could tackle each identified challenge.

Blood Container Ideation   


Users needed to be able to sample a few drops of blood and load them into the device for analysis. The blood container was one of the crucial components of the testing journey. We had the aim of improving the sampling journey so that users with limited dexterity could easily handle the blood container without much supervision.

Iterative Design


Our approach was iterative from the start. Having had experience designing a similar product previously, we understood how complex it can be to successfully land on a design that allows precise and effortless blood sampling.


We needed to experiment and test different geometries in order to find the shape that allowed us to have the strongest capillary force.

This meant that we needed to find a way to prototype parts that had very tight dimensions in a fast and cost effective manner. We used a combination of manufacturing methods, including 3D printing, lamination and digital cutting to prototype and test shapes and geometries that tested our different concepts and hypotheses.

Early User Testing


We explored multiple ways of interacting with the blood container. We brought non-working prototypes to focus group sessions to see how users would hold and handle them.

Mainly through observation we could identify features that worked better and using questionnaires we made sure to understand the reasons for any discomfort or preferences.

Testing at an early stage allowed us to understand patients' mental models around the blood sampling journey.

We took this feedback back to the drawing board and refined the different concepts to include usability insights from our previous testing.



User Testing & Refinement   


After having experimented and tested with a variety of geometries and having agreed on which concept was the most efficient at collecting the blood sample we opened up ideation again, this time to explore handling at a deeper level and make sure we had addressed usability holistically.


Under usability were actively considering features such as thickness, width, blood spilling, locating the aperture and indicating when to stop collecting blood.

Through our tests we could narrow down the concepts into 3 different proposals that were prototyped to a higher fidelity using vacuum casting.

User Testing Amidst COVID-19


All throughout the project, covid-19 presented us with many challenges accessing patients at hospitals and at home. We had foreseen these issues and established a close connection with a partner hospital in Manchester and recruited health professionals into our research team to help us with the different phases of the study.

Nevertheless, we also needed to reach patients at home and be able to talk to them in a non-clinical environment. The consequent tests were conducted as online interviews, and for these we shipped all the equipment needed (cameras, props and models) to patients in England and Wales.


During the last phase of development of the blood container we tackled CMF aspects that could enhance usability even further. This included features such as obscuring any other components or geometries on the inside of the container that were not crucial to the user and highlighting the aperture with pad printing.

Final Design


Testing different levels of fidelity with users and prototyping functioning models with manufacturers allowed us to arrive on a design that was able to easily capture the blood sample from any angle. Some of the other functions the blood container had to fulfil were: avoid overfilling and underfilling, facilitate blood capture and de-risk blockage.

Enclosure Ideation   


The enclosure ideation phase was run in parallel to the development of the blood container. We explored ways of interacting with the main loading area for the blood container, accessing the internals, screen location and handling, among other usability aspects.


The nature of the platform meant that the device needed to live at the patient's home. We wanted to keep the aesthetic of the device discrete and as minimal as possible.

We explored the visual landscape of home products and clustered products using different moodboards. These helped inform our decisions while prototyping and testing.


At this stage we had gone through a series of collaborative sessions with design engineers to understand the possibilities and limitations of the internal hardware architecture. Doing this allowed us to start sketching out concepts having feasible layouts in mind.


Integration with the blood container took place in parallel to prototyping these enclosure concepts. Emphasis was made on the ergonomics and human factors of loading the container into the device.

We had a clear set of recorded user needs that specified the container to be handled in any direction and to be left and right hand agnostic. Through testing we were able to identify the set of parameters that affected usability and kept them as considerations when looking at the blood container-device interface design for manufacturing. .

Enclosure Ideation   


Through the ideation phase we refined the concepts into two main proposals that summarised the possible architecture of components and interactions. These models were only aimed for usability purposes and UX studies.

Initial Prototypes


These two concepts had different sizes and had different screen locations. We tested portability, door interaction, screen interaction and weight.

Branding elements were not considered yet since they were still being formed at the time. We kept the colour to a monochrome palette with the exception of colour highlights where we thought they would aid the overall UX.

Remote Testing


The two selected models were shipped to recruited users' homes for testing. We had to account for filming equipment and included a set of instructions for them to set up the cameras.

The user would have a gopro camera with a mounting arm and a base, a laptop or phone and the rest of props and models we wanted them to test. Testing was recorded through video calls on the user's laptop or phone and also on a top bird's view on the gopro camera.

We would have two members of the team participate on the call. The first member would act as facilitator and lead the user through the exercises and questions while the second member of the team would take notes.

CMF & Manufacturing   


The different phases of testing allowed us to refine the concepts and incorporate usability learnings into the final design. Having the hardware architecture and core UX defined we could move into working with detailing and finishes specifications with manufacturers. The design for manufacturing phase focused on getting all parts of the enclosure to a good design stage for injection moulding.

Manfacturing Development


Due to pressure on the project deadlines and delays in some parts of manufacturing supplies globally some compromises had to be taken to be able to meet deadlines.

This meant the device couldn't reach its final design specs but minor changes had to be made that didn't impact core UX. These delays mostly affected the procurement of internal electrical components including the bespoke screen which meant that the device ended up gaining a few millimetres in overall height and the use of an readily available OTS screen.

DFM


The DFM process was split in two phases. In the first phase the device is manufactured in low quantities through vacuum casting processes in order to perform clinical validation and verification purposes.

In the second and final phase the device is manufactured in the right material and through injection moulding for mass production.
